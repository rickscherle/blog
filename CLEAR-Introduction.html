Part One

The problem isn't you. It's the tools.

I spent most of my life knowing I was smart. I could see problems clearly, generate ideas quickly, and talk about what needed to be done with real precision. What I couldn't do — what baffled me and everyone around me — was actually do the things I said I was going to do. Not the big things. The ordinary things. The Tuesday afternoon things.

I wasn't diagnosed with ADHD until I was in my late fifties. And the diagnosis didn't just explain my struggles — it reframed my entire life. The piles on my desk, the unfinished projects, the promises I'd made and hadn't kept — these weren't character defects. They were symptoms of a neurological condition that nobody had thought to look for until it was almost too late to matter.

"The person with ADHD isn't lazy or undisciplined. They're running without the neurological scaffolding that other people use unconsciously."

Dr. Russell Barkley — the leading researcher in the field — describes ADHD not as an attention disorder but as a disorder of self-regulation and working memory, combined with a phenomenon he calls time blindness. The future doesn't feel real. Deadlines that are two weeks away are motivationally invisible. The only time that carries any weight is right now.

That's the condition. And when you understand it that clearly, you realize immediately why every productivity system you've ever tried has failed you.

Why the tools always fail

Every major productivity system on the market — Todoist, Things, Notion, paper planners, GTD — was designed by and for neurotypical people. They assume you can do things that ADHD specifically impairs:

  • Hold time in your mind
    These systems show you clock times — "meeting at 2pm." But time blindness means "2pm" has no felt weight until it's 1:58 and you're in the wrong building.
  • Choose from a long list
    A 47-item to-do list is not a productivity tool for someone with ADHD. It's a wall of equally urgent noise that produces paralysis, not action.
  • Self-initiate without a prompt
    These systems wait for you to remember to check them. But the entire problem with ADHD is that you can't reliably remember to check anything.
  • Maintain discipline after falling off
    When you miss a day — or a week — conventional systems confront you with a backlog of failure. That shame spiral makes picking the system back up feel impossible.

The abandoned to-do app isn't evidence that you're broken. It's evidence of a product-market mismatch. The tool was built for someone else's brain.

Part Two

A different approach, built from the neuroscience up

I built CLEAR after my diagnosis because I couldn't find anything that actually addressed what my brain needed. Not a better task list. Not a prettier calendar. A system that understood the specific way ADHD impairs function and compensated for it directly.

The result is built on one central insight from Barkley's research: the ADHD brain isn't broken. It's differently powered. It runs on interest, urgency, challenge, and novelty. A system that fights those properties will always fail. A system that harnesses them can change everything.

CLEAR acts as what I think of as an external prefrontal cortex — the part of the brain responsible for self-regulation, planning, and working memory. When yours doesn't reliably show up, the system shows up instead.

The CLEAR framework

The system is organized around five principles that correspond to the five letters:

C
Capture Everything
A single inbox where everything goes immediately — by voice, text, email, or photo. Your brain stops holding things and starts processing them.
L
Limit Ruthlessly
Once a week, you set three — and only three — outcomes that would make the week a success. Not tasks. Outcomes. The system holds everything else.
E
Execute on Context
The system knows your energy level and available time and shows you only the tasks that fit right now. No decisions. Just start.
A
Account for Time
Time is made physical and visible. Countdowns replace clock times. Your sovereign time — the hours that are genuinely yours — is shown on every screen.
R
Review and Reset
A ten-minute daily close resolves every open loop before you sleep. A weekly planning session sets the week's intention. The system never lets things fall through the cracks.
Part Three

What it actually looks like to use it

Here's what a day with CLEAR feels like in practice. I'll walk you through the key moments.

The moment a thought hits you

It's 7am. You're making coffee and you remember that the ranch water pump has been making a noise. In the past, that thought would live in your head all day, generating background anxiety, until you either dealt with it urgently or forgot it entirely.

With CLEAR, you say: "Hey Siri, add to CLEAR inbox: water pump on the ranch is making a grinding noise, probably needs service before winter." Done. The thought is out of your head and safe in the system in under ten seconds. You go back to your coffee.

Ending the day with a clear head

At 5:30pm, CLEAR sends you a notification: "Time to close out the day. Ten minutes and your head will be clear." You run through a five-screen flow that takes about ten minutes. You log what you actually did, route anything incomplete to tomorrow or someday, and — critically — you decide right now what tomorrow's Most Important Task will be.

When you're done, the system tells you: "You're finished for today. The system will hold everything until morning." The badge clears. The app goes quiet. Tomorrow's first decision is already made.

For someone with ADHD, that last part is not a small thing. The overnight anxiety of unresolved open loops is one of the most debilitating features of the condition. The daily close closes those loops deliberately. You sleep better because the system is holding what your brain no longer has to.

Part Four

What changes when the system works

I want to be honest with you: CLEAR is not a cure. ADHD is neurological, and no app changes your neurology. What CLEAR does is provide the external scaffolding that compensates for the internal scaffolding that isn't reliably there.

Here's what that actually means in day-to-day life:

The background anxiety quiets
When everything is captured and held safely, you stop carrying it in your head. The low-level hum of "what am I forgetting" starts to fade.
Time becomes visible
The time bar makes the shape of your day physical. You stop being surprised by how little time you actually have — and stop being surprised by how much you do.
Your own goals get protected
The system advocates for your self-directed work, not just your obligations. The book doesn't keep getting crowded out by everyone else's urgencies.
Falling off doesn't mean failing
When you miss a day or a week, the system recovers gracefully. There's no wall of failure waiting for you. Just a path forward.
Optional coaching that actually helps
CLEAR includes an optional coach dashboard — a read-only view of your patterns that a professional ADHD coach can use to make your weekly sessions grounded in what actually happened, not what you remember happening. The methodology is embedded in the software, but a skilled coach makes it work much faster.
Part Five

A note to the people who love someone with ADHD

If you're reading this because someone in your life has ADHD — a partner, a child, a parent — I want to say something directly to you.

What you've been watching isn't laziness. It isn't indifference. It isn't a choice. The person you love is running a race in shoes that don't fit, on a track that wasn't designed for them, and the cruelest part is that they look fine from the outside. So the failure is attributed to character rather than circumstance.

CLEAR can help — but it is not a solution you can give someone. It is a system a person has to choose. The most useful thing a loved one can do is understand what the condition actually is, stop interpreting failure as a moral statement, and be curious rather than frustrated when the system doesn't immediately fix everything.

There is also a specific way this system can help your relationship: for the first time, you will both be looking at the same honest picture of what is happening. Not your interpretation of it. Not their memory of it. The actual data. That shared reality is something many couples and families with ADHD in the picture have never had before.
