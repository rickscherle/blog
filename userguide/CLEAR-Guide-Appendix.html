Appendix

Glossary and Reference

Every term CLEAR uses, defined in one place. Plus quick-reference tables for the daily and weekly rituals, inbox processing paths, fit score components, and the core design principles that shape every product decision.

AB CD EF HI LM NP RS TW

Glossary

A
Activation difficulty
A task field that classifies how hard it is to begin the task — not how hard the task itself is. Three values: Easy (you can start immediately), Medium (requires setup or a decision), Hard (strong resistance or complexity at the moment of starting). Used by the fit score to match tasks to your current state.
Anchor mode
Controls how the next due date is calculated when a recurring task is completed. Calendar schedules the next instance from the original anchor date regardless of when you finished. Completion schedules the next instance from the date you completed this one. Use Calendar for fixed obligations; use Completion for maintenance tasks where the interval matters more than the date.
Architecture Session
See Weekly Architecture Session.
B
Big Three
The three most important outcomes you're committed to completing in the current week, set during the Weekly Architecture Session. Not tasks — outcomes. The Big Three provide direction for the Launchpad and grant a +25 fit score boost to all tasks in any linked project. Only one or two Big Three outcomes are set during re-engagement after a break.
Buffer zone
A protected window of time before and after a calendar event — for travel, transition, and recovery. Buffer zones appear on the time strip as muted fills. Tasks are not scheduled into buffer zones by the fit score algorithm.
C
Cadence
A connection frequency set for a family member or close friend — weekly, monthly, or quarterly. When the cadence interval passes without a logged connection, a low-priority prompt appears: "You haven't connected with [Name] in a while. Want to reach out?"
Capture
The act of getting something out of your head and into CLEAR's inbox. Capture should be as fast and frictionless as possible — voice, text, or document. Nothing is decided at capture time. Deciding happens during processing.
CLEAR
The acronym that defines the five-phase system: Capture everything. Limit ruthlessly. Execute on context. Account for time. Review and reset. Also the name of the app.
Context filtering
The algorithm that decides which tasks appear in your Now list at any given moment. It scores every active task against your current energy level, time available, location, and other factors, then surfaces the highest-scoring tasks. Formally called the fit score algorithm.
Context notes
A freeform living field on each contact record — a few lines of what's currently true and relevant about this person. Surfaces automatically when the contact appears in a task, project, or appointment view. Updated by tapping "Anything worth remembering?" after completing a task involving the contact.
D
Daily Close
The evening ritual that closes out the workday. Runs for approximately five minutes. Shows completed and deferred tasks, sets tomorrow's MIT, and ends with a capture prompt to offload any remaining open loops. Tracked as a streak.
Deferred count
The number of times a task has been moved out of today's active list without being completed. At three deferrals, the system surfaces the "Who Does This Serve?" prompt before allowing another. At repeated deferrals, the system may surface an activation interview.
Dependency
A hard relationship between two tasks where one cannot begin until the other is complete. A task with a dependency is hidden from all views — including the Launchpad — until the blocking task is marked done. When the blocker completes, the dependent task activates automatically.
E
Energy level (morning)
Your self-reported cognitive state at the start of the day, set during Morning Setup. Four options: High (ready for deep work), Medium (can focus but not for long), Low (need easy wins), Fragmented (scattered day of small slots). Shapes the fit score for the entire day by matching task energy requirements to your actual capacity.
Energy required
A task field classifying the cognitive demand of the work. Three values: Deep (sustained concentration, creative or analytical work), Medium (engaged but not depleted), Admin (low-cognitive routine work). Matched against your morning energy level by the fit score.
Errand run
A cluster of tasks sharing the same physical location, grouped together so they can be planned and executed as a single outing. Detected automatically when three or more active tasks share a location context, or created manually. Tasks within an errand run can have dependencies that enforce a sequence.
F
Fit score
A number computed for every active task that reflects how well it matches your current context — energy, time available, location, stakes, and other factors. The Now list on the Launchpad shows the tasks with the highest fit scores right now. The score is never shown to you; only its result (what surfaces) is visible.
Focus Mode
A distraction-free environment that launches when you tap a task to begin working. Shows only the task, a countdown ring, session progress, and optionally a relevant file. All other views are hidden. Sessions default to 25 minutes. Completed sessions generate a difficulty check and time confirmation before returning to the Launchpad.
H
Hat
A distinct life area — a context with its own relationships, obligations, and definition of "on top of things." Every task, project, appointment, and contact belongs to a hat. Maximum seven active hats. The hat system drives the color coding throughout the app and feeds the neglect detection algorithm.
Hat neglect detection
A background algorithm that tracks engagement across your hats using a recency-weighted activity score. Surfaces observations — not instructions — in the Weekly Architecture Session hat pulse screen, the progress dashboard, and (for critically neglected hats only) a single weekly notification. Never interrupts the Launchpad or a focus session.
Horizon
A project's time frame: This week, This month, This quarter, or Someday. Shapes how frequently the project surfaces during planning. A This Week project is reviewed every Weekly Architecture Session. A Someday project is reviewed monthly.
I
Inbox
The single collection point for everything you capture. Nothing in the inbox is prioritized or categorized — it's a raw holding area. Items move out of the inbox only through processing, which routes each item to its correct destination: task, project, appointment, reminder, Someday, or trash.
Inbox processing
The act of reviewing captured items one at a time and routing each to its correct destination. Six paths: Task, Reminder, Appointment, Park it (Someday), Project, Trash it. Processing is where decisions are made. Capture is intentionally decision-free.
L
Launchpad
The main workday screen. Shows your MIT at the top, the time strip, and the Now list — the three to five tasks best matched to your current context. Everything else is hidden by design. The Launchpad is not a task list; it is a decision surface that answers the question: what do I do next?
Lead time
For recurring tasks: the number of days before a due date that the instance enters your active task pool and becomes visible in the Launchpad. Default is three days. A task with a lead time of seven days will surface a week before it's due, giving you time to complete it without urgency.
M
MIT
Most Important Task. The one task that, if completed, would make the day a success regardless of what else happens. Set during Morning Setup. Pinned to the top of the Launchpad. MIT completion rate — the percentage of days you complete your MIT — is tracked in the dashboard.
Morning Setup
The brief three-screen ritual that starts each workday. Step 1: energy check. Step 2: heads-up review of upcoming events in the next three days. Step 3: MIT selection. Takes two to three minutes. Sets the parameters the Launchpad uses to filter tasks for the entire day.
N
Next commitment arc
The circular countdown indicator on the Launchpad showing time until your next calendar commitment. Green when you have plenty of time, shifting to red as the commitment approaches. Hidden when the next commitment is more than 12 hours away.
Now list
The three to five tasks shown below the MIT on the Launchpad — the highest-scoring tasks for your current context. Not a complete task list. Not a priority ranking. A curated set of the right tasks for right now. The "Got 5 min? 3 easy wins" banner at the bottom offers quick Admin tasks when you have a short gap.
P
Park it
One of the six inbox processing paths. Sends a captured item to Someday — acknowledged as real, but not timely. No setup required. The item surfaces in the monthly Someday review.
Processing queue
A holding state for items that have been acknowledged but not yet fully processed — for example, inbox items deferred during a re-engagement flow, or imported tasks that received only a hat assignment during migration. Items in the processing queue are visible but have minimal metadata and will eventually need full processing.
R
Recovery mode
A streak mechanic that allows a broken streak to be partially restored. After a break in a Daily Close or MIT streak, completing three consecutive days activates Recovery, which marks the streak with a gap indicator rather than resetting it to zero. More honest than pretending the absence didn't happen; more forgiving than loss.
Re-engagement flow
The structured return sequence that fires when you open CLEAR after an extended absence. Five steps: silent cleanup of expired data, inbox quick pass, triage of what still matters, fresh week setup (one or two outcomes), and today's MIT. Designed to make coming back feel like the right decision — never to confront you with accumulated failure.
S
Significant date
A date linked to a contact — birthday, anniversary, work anniversary, or other. Automatically generates a recurring annual reminder that fires seven days before the date with an action prompt: "Sarah's birthday is in 7 days. Want to add anything for it?"
Someday
A low-pressure holding area for ideas and commitments that are real but not yet timely. Completely segregated from the active task pool — Someday items never appear in the Launchpad. Reviewed monthly via a prompted one-at-a-time flow: Keep Parked, Make Active, or Delete.
Sovereign time
Discretionary time that belongs to you — windows in the day not consumed by external commitments, appointments, or maintenance. Shown as the second bar on the time strip. The Launchpad summary "3 sovereign windows · 7.2 hrs yours" tells you how much of the day is genuinely available for self-directed work.
Stakes
A task field classifying the consequences of not doing it. Three values: Relational (someone else is directly affected; social consequences are immediate), Functional (life degrades without it, but slowly), Personal (only you are affected; no external enforcement). Used silently by the fit score and conflict detection — never shown as a judgment.
T
Task Iceberg
The principle that every task or appointment has a visible portion — the event itself — and a hidden portion that includes preparation time, travel there, travel back, and follow-up. Schedules collapse when planning accounts only for the visible tip. CLEAR's buffer zones address the transition layers; the travel and follow-up layers require honest time accounting when creating the appointment. See Chapters 10 and 13 for the full treatment.
Time origin
A task field classifying who generated the commitment. Three values: Self-directed (something you want to build or accomplish), Externally obligated (required by another person or institution), Life maintenance (necessary background tasks that keep life functioning). Used by the Time Sovereignty Map and conflict detection.
Time sovereignty
The concept that your time has three categories — self-directed, externally obligated, and life maintenance — and that understanding the ratio between them is as important as managing individual tasks. The Time Sovereignty Map in the progress dashboard shows where your discretionary time has actually been going.
Time strip
The two-bar visual at the top of the Launchpad that makes your day physically visible. The top bar shows your full day — committed events and elapsed time. The bottom bar shows only your sovereign windows. A single vertical line marks the current moment across both bars. Time labels run along the bottom from your workday start to end.
W
Weekly Architecture Session
The once-weekly planning ritual — typically Sunday evening — that sets the Big Three outcomes, reviews hat health, ensures no life area is being neglected, and prepares the week ahead. Takes 20–30 minutes when done consistently. The single most important ritual in the CLEAR system. Tracked as a streak.
Who does this serve?
A prompt that fires when a task has been deferred three or more times. Asks you to honestly identify whether the task is something you want to do, something you have to do, or something that's no longer real. The goal is not guilt — it's surfacing the underlying reason for avoidance so it can be addressed directly.

Quick reference

The daily and weekly rituals

Morning Setup
Each workday · 2–3 min
Energy check → Heads-up review → MIT selection. Sets the parameters that shape your entire day.
Daily Close
Each workday evening · 5 min
Review completions and deferrals → Set tomorrow's MIT → Final capture. Offloads open loops before sleep.
Someday Review
Monthly · 10 min
One item at a time: Keep Parked, Make Active, or Delete. Keeps the list honest and small.
Weekly Architecture
Weekly · 20–30 min
Retrospective → Inbox clear → Hat pulse → Big Three → Calendar review → Maintenance window. The most important ritual in the system.

Inbox processing paths

A — Task
One concrete action, done once
Full eight-field setup: title, hat, energy, time estimate, time origin, stakes, location, activation difficulty. Enters active task pool and begins scoring.
B — Reminder
A date or occasion to be aware of
Creates a ClearEvent with a reminder. Does not consume sovereign time. Optionally prompts "Want to do anything for this?" when the date arrives.
C — Appointment
A time-specific commitment
Creates a CalendarEvent with buffer zones. Syncs to your connected calendar. Appears on the time strip and consumes sovereign time.
D — Park it
Real but not timely
Sends the item to Someday. No setup required. Reviewed monthly. Never appears in the Launchpad.
E — Project
A multi-step outcome
Full nine-step project setup: outcome statement, hat, horizon, deadline, weekly progress target, first action, additional steps, people involved, review. First action enters the active task pool immediately.
F — Trash it
Not real
Deleted. Recoverable for 30 days. The system frames it plainly: "Good call."

Core design principles

P1
Minimize visible complexity at all times
Default views show 3–7 items. The system filters — users cannot be trusted to filter their own view.
P2
Decide for the user wherever possible
Pre-select, pre-filter, pre-prioritize. Present choices only as a last resort — and then only 2–3 options.
P3
Asymmetric friction
Capture is nearly zero friction. Accessing overwhelming views requires deliberate navigation. The default path always leads to "what do I do next."
P4
Make time physical and visible
Countdown rings, progress arcs, and visual time bars supplement or replace clock times. The system assumes the user cannot feel time passing.
P5
The system reaches out
Passive systems fail ADHD users. The system pushes relevant information to the user at the right moment rather than waiting to be checked.
P6
Never punish falling off
After an absence, handle the backlog gracefully, present a path forward, and never confront the user with accumulated failure. Streaks are recoverable.
P7
Speak like a coach
Warm, direct, encouraging — not corporate. Error states, empty states, and prompts all follow this voice.
P8
Context is everything
The same task list is not appropriate at all times. The system knows what context the user is in and filters accordingly.
