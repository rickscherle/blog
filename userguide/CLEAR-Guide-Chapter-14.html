Your Calendar and Time

The Launchpad is where you execute. The calendar is where you understand. It shows the full picture of your time — what's committed, what's free, what's genuinely yours — and gives you the tools to protect the space for the work that matters.

The three calendar views

The calendar is accessible from the Calendar tab in the bottom navigation. Three views are available, each suited to a different cognitive mode.

Day View Default during workday
A vertical timeline of the current day. The current time is shown as a prominent horizontal line — you always know exactly where you are in the day. Appointments and time blocks appear as colored bands. Sovereign windows are highlighted between commitments. Countdown chips for the next two upcoming events are always visible at the top. This is the view for situational awareness during the day — you can see at a glance how much time is left, what's coming, and where the open windows are.
Week View
A compact seven-column layout showing the full week. Your Big Three outcomes for the week appear as a banner at the top. Events are color-coded by hat. Unblocked time — white space — is displayed in a distinct color to make available time visible without requiring you to read every event. This is the view for planning: seeing where your week has room, spotting clusters of obligations, and understanding why certain days feel heavier than others.
Agenda View
A pure chronological list of events and time blocks — no empty time shown, only commitments. This is the view for low-energy states when you need a clean answer to "what's actually on my calendar?" without visual noise. Useful before a meeting to confirm what's next, or at the end of the day to see what's coming tomorrow.
Switching views

The calendar remembers your last-used view within the session. During the workday, it defaults to Day View. On weekends and during the Weekly Architecture Session, it tends toward Week View. Switch between them freely — they all show the same underlying data, just framed differently.

Time blocks — reserving time for your work

A time block is a reserved window in your calendar for focused work. Unlike an appointment, it has no external party — it's a commitment you make to yourself. Creating a time block tells the system: this window is taken. No tasks will be scheduled over it without a warning. The fit score treats it as committed time.

Creating a time block

Tap and hold on any open time slot in Day or Week view. A time block creation sheet appears. Set a label (Deep Work, Admin, Creative, Writing — whatever fits), the energy type that matches the work you're planning, and optionally a specific task to pre-assign to the block.

The minimum block size is 25 minutes — less than that isn't a meaningful focused window. If you try to create a block that would eliminate the buffer zone after an existing calendar event, the system flags it: "This block would leave no transition time before your 2pm appointment. Create it anyway?" You can override, but you do so knowingly.

Pre-assigning a task

When you create a time block, you can optionally assign an existing task to it. This doesn't change the task's status or score — it simply adds context to the block. When the time arrives, the block on the Day View shows the assigned task prominently, and tapping the block launches Focus Mode on that task directly. It removes one decision from the moment the block starts.

Time blocks vs. appointments

Both consume time on the time strip and reduce sovereign windows. The difference is origin: appointments are commitments to external parties, time blocks are commitments to yourself. This distinction matters for conflict detection — the system treats encroachment on your self-directed time differently from a scheduling conflict between two external commitments.

Remember the iceberg. When you create a time block for a task, account for more than the task itself. A 90-minute writing session realistically needs 10–15 minutes of ramp-up and some wind-down time. An appointment that runs until 3pm may take until 3:45 to fully recover from — travel back, notes filed, follow-up tasks created. The time strip shows what's blocked. Only you know what the full cost actually is. See Chapter 13 for the full treatment of the task iceberg.

Sovereign windows and the time sovereignty map

The Day View renders a Time Sovereignty Map as a visual layer over the standard calendar. The algorithm works through the day and identifies every window of time that isn't claimed by an external obligation or its buffers. These windows — your sovereign time — are highlighted in teal.

Windows shorter than 20 minutes don't count. A 15-minute gap between a meeting and another commitment is transition time, not directable time. The system is honest about what's actually usable.

A summary header at the top of the Day View shows the result: "You have 3 sovereign windows today — about 4.5 hours to direct." This number is the honest picture of your day. Not the hours between 8am and 6pm. Not the hours when you're not in meetings. The hours that are genuinely available for directed work.

Most people have no idea how much sovereign time they actually have on any given day. The number is usually smaller than they think — and the shape of it is usually more fragmented. Seeing it honestly is the first step toward protecting it deliberately.

Protecting self-directed time

During the Weekly Architecture Session, after you've set your Big Three, the time sovereignty screen shows next week's calendar and asks you to assign at least one time block to each Big Three outcome before anything else. This is the pay-yourself-first principle made structural: your goals get protected windows before the week fills with other things.

Time blocks created this way are flagged as self-directed and are visually distinguished in the calendar view — a teal border or similar treatment that signals: this window is for you, not for anyone else.

Conflict detection

CLEAR watches for two kinds of conflict: hard conflicts, where something is scheduled over something else in a way that's physically impossible, and soft conflicts, where a pattern is emerging that's eroding your self-directed time without any single obvious collision.

Hard conflicts

A hard conflict occurs when an external event is added — via calendar sync or manual entry — that encroaches on an existing self-directed time block. The system surfaces an alert immediately:

Hard conflict detected
"Your deep work block from 3–5 PM conflicts with baseball pickup at 4:15 and its buffer. Want to shorten the block to end at 3:45, or move it?"
Shorten to 3:45
Move the block
Keep both — I'll handle it

You can shorten the block, move it, or keep both and handle the overlap yourself. The system doesn't make the decision for you — it surfaces the conflict and presents options. Choosing "Keep both" is a valid choice; sometimes life is genuinely that compressed. But you make that choice explicitly rather than discovering it when you're already late.

Soft conflicts

A soft conflict is a pattern, not a collision. It's what happens when external obligations consistently displace your self-directed work across multiple days — not because of any single event but because of the accumulated weight of everything else.

The system tracks when self-directed time blocks are interrupted, overwritten, or marked incomplete. When this happens on three or more consecutive days, an alert surfaces in the Daily Close and as a weekly coaching insight:

Pattern detected
"This is the fourth day this week that your personal work time was displaced by other commitments. Your Big Three outcome 'Chapter 4 draft complete' hasn't had any protected time since Monday. Want to look at next week's calendar together?"

This alert doesn't assign blame. It surfaces a pattern that ADHD time blindness makes invisible — because the brain experiences each day independently and can't accumulate the multi-day picture without help. The system can. And it tells you what it sees.

The soft conflict alert doesn't try to fix the problem for you. It asks one question: do you want to look at next week's calendar together? The answer might be yes — and then you go into the time sovereignty screen and protect the time. Or the answer might be no — and you continue, but now knowingly.

The system advocates for your self-directed goals because no one else will. External obligations enforce themselves — missed meetings have consequences. Personal goals don't. The soft conflict system is the mechanism CLEAR uses to act as an external accountability structure for the work that matters only to you.

When something is due today but has no scheduled time

A task with a due date of today but no time on the calendar is one of the most dangerous situations in the system — especially for someone with time blindness. It's urgent, it's real, and it's invisible on the time strip. Without intervention, it quietly becomes overdue while the rest of the day fills up around it.

CLEAR handles this proactively. At your configured workday start time — even if you haven't opened the app — the system finds any tasks due today with no scheduled time and places them on your calendar automatically. It picks the first available sovereign window that's large enough for the task, adds a buffer for the parts of the task you probably didn't fully account for, and makes sure the block ends before your workday does.

You'll get a notification: "I've scheduled [task title] for 10:30am — it's due today." The block appears on your time strip with a small clock indicator so you know the system placed it, not you. You can move it, resize it, or take it off the calendar entirely — but it's there, visible, taking up real space in your day.

If you complete the morning setup, due-today tasks appear on their own screen before the MIT confirmation — with a one-tap option to schedule each one or dismiss it. Dismissed tasks don't get auto-scheduled, but the system keeps watching.

What happens if the day fills up

If there's genuinely no time in your day to fit a due-today task — every sovereign window is too short or already blocked — the system tells you immediately. Not at 5pm. Right now, when there's still a chance to do something about it. The alert surfaces three options: move something to make room, flag it for escalation (contact someone, ask for an extension), or acknowledge and handle it yourself.

The intervention ladder

Even with a block on the calendar, due-today tasks can get lost — especially if the day goes sideways. CLEAR watches for this and intervenes at two points:

At the midpoint of your workday — if the task is still open and unstarted, a notification fires: "[Task title] is due today and hasn't been started. You have [N] hours left." If your scheduled block has already passed without being touched, the notification offers to reschedule into whatever time remains.

90 minutes before your workday ends — if the task is still open, a high-priority notification fires with one tap to launch Focus Mode directly on the task. No browsing, no decision. If the originally scheduled block has already passed uncompleted, this warning fires immediately rather than waiting for the 90-minute mark.

At the Daily Close

If a due-today task reaches the Daily Close uncompleted, it gets its own screen — not grouped with routine incompletes. You're presented with three options: mark it done, escalate it (contact someone, ask for an extension, capture what needs to happen), or move it and acknowledge it missed its deadline. Missed-deadline tasks are flagged in the following Sunday's Weekly Architecture Session so the pattern is visible.

The system acts whether or not you show up to the morning setup. Time blindness means a due-today task with no scheduled time can vanish into the day without trace. CLEAR doesn't wait for you to notice. It schedules, it reminds, and it escalates — because that's what an external prefrontal cortex is supposed to do.

What if you add a due-today task mid-day?

The morning process only knows about tasks that existed when your workday started. If you create or process a task during the day and give it a due date of today — in inbox processing, in a project view, anywhere — CLEAR responds immediately. It doesn't wait for the next scheduled trigger.

The moment the task is saved, the system looks at what sovereign time is still available and finds the right window — not the one you're in right now, which would just interrupt what you're already doing, but the next natural break point. If you just created a task while you're in the middle of something, the system respects that and waits.

For short tasks — under 15 minutes — CLEAR skips the formal time block entirely and adds the task to your Quick Admin section instead. A notification tells you it's there. For longer tasks, it finds a window later in the day, skips the immediately upcoming one if it's too close, and places a block where you'll actually have room to transition into it properly.

If the available time is tight — say the task is 90 minutes and you only have 45 minutes left in your largest free window — the system schedules it anyway but tells you the time may not be enough. If there's genuinely no time left in the workday, the no-room escalation fires immediately: the same three-option prompt as the morning version, but with more urgency because there's less runway to solve it.

One small note: if you're mid-session — processing your inbox, running the Weekly Architecture Session — the alert queues rather than interrupting you. It fires when the session completes. A badge on the Launchpad tab signals that something needs attention.

Calendar sync

CLEAR connects to Google Calendar or Apple Calendar during onboarding and maintains a continuous bidirectional sync. Events you add to your external calendar appear in CLEAR. Events you create as appointments or time blocks in CLEAR appear in your external calendar. The connection is live — changes propagate within a few seconds on either side.

When events arrive from an external calendar, CLEAR classifies them automatically. Events with a specific start time, duration, and location are treated as appointments — they land on the time strip with buffer zones. All-day events, imported holidays, and birthday events from Google Contacts are treated as awareness-only — they appear in the calendar view as indicators but don't affect the time strip or sovereign window computation.

Multiple calendars

If you have both a work calendar and a personal calendar, CLEAR can connect both. All events merge into a single timeline, with each event tagged to its source calendar. CLEAR doesn't distinguish between work and personal in any meaningful way — both feed into your sovereign window computation, because both consume real time from your real day.

Weather-aware scheduling — coming in a future version

If you have tasks tagged with an outdoor or ranch location context, you may have noticed that the system surfaces them based on your manual location context setting — but it doesn't yet know whether it's raining at the ranch.

Weather-aware scheduling is designed for a future version. When it arrives, tasks tagged as outdoors or ranch will be automatically suppressed on days when the forecast shows rain, snow, or extreme temperatures. On days with favorable weather — especially when the forecast shows conditions deteriorating in the coming days — outdoor tasks will receive a scoring boost.

The Weekly Architecture Session will gain a weather awareness layer: during the time sovereignty screen, favorable weather windows for outdoor work will be highlighted, so you can assign ranch or outdoor time blocks to the days when conditions are actually good.
