The Weekly Architecture Session

Once a week, you step back from the daily rhythm and look at the larger picture. The Weekly Architecture Session takes about 45 minutes. It clears the inbox, reviews your projects, checks in on your hats, sets three outcomes for the week, and protects the time to actually pursue them. When it's done, you know exactly what the week is for.

When it runs

The session is triggered by a notification at your configured time — Sunday evening by default, at 7:00 PM. The notification says: "Time for your weekly planning session. 45 minutes now saves 10 hours of confusion this week." You can also open it manually at any time from the Me tab.

The session is a linear wizard — seven screens in sequence. You can't skip screens, but you can exit and resume exactly where you left off. If something interrupts you mid-session, nothing is lost. Pick it back up when you're ready.

On timing

Sunday evening works well for most people because it creates a clean mental boundary between the week just ending and the week beginning. But the right time is when you'll actually do it. Some people prefer Friday afternoon as the old week closes. Others prefer Monday morning before the day starts. Whatever you set, the system will prompt you then — consistently, not repeatedly if you decline.

The seven screens

1
Last week's retrospective
Your Big Three outcomes from last week are shown. For each one, a single tap: Yes, Partially, or No. No text required. The session summarizes: "You hit 2 of 3 outcomes last week. That's solid." The retrospective exists not to grade you but to close the loop on last week before turning attention to the next one. What you measure honestly, you learn from.
~5 minutes
2
Inbox to zero
If your inbox has unprocessed items, the processing flow runs here — the same six-path fate decision from Chapter 3, one item at a time. A progress indicator shows where you are: "5 of 12 processed." If you'd rather not process everything right now, a Defer remaining button batch-moves all unreviewed items to Someday. The goal is to enter the week with a clear inbox — nothing unacknowledged waiting.
~variable
3
Active project review
Each active project is shown as a card: title, next action, last updated. Projects with a hard external deadline come first, sorted by days remaining, with a pace status indicator — "Due Mar 15 · 18 days · ⚠ Behind." For each project, a quick-tap decision: On Track, Stalled, Done, or Park to Someday. Stalled projects immediately prompt: "What is the very next physical action?" — forcing the project back into motion before you move on. Any project at risk within two weeks of its deadline gets a warning card requiring an explicit response before you continue.
~5 minutes
4
The hat pulse
Each of your active hats is shown as a card, sorted by how long it's been since any task in that hat was completed. The neglect detection algorithm has already run — hats quiet for an extended period carry status indicators, and the prompts adapt accordingly. A hat with normal activity gets: "How is your Ranch hat this week?" A hat that hasn't moved in three weeks gets: "Ranch hasn't had any movement in 23 days. What's going on there?" Each hat has an optional text field. Tap to skip if there's nothing to note.
~5 minutes
5
Set the Big Three
Full-screen. Three text fields. One question: "What three outcomes would make next week a genuine success?" The system offers auto-suggestions based on overdue high-priority tasks, stalled projects, and hat neglect signals — but you write the outcomes in your own words. After each one, the system checks whether it connects to an active project and offers to link them. Tasks inside a linked project receive a fit score boost throughout the week.
~10 minutes
6
Time sovereignty
Next week's calendar is shown with all externally-obligated events plotted and sovereign windows highlighted. The screen opens with: "Here is what next week actually looks like. Your free windows are highlighted." This view cannot be skipped — it's the factual foundation for everything else. For each Big Three outcome, you're asked: "When will you work on this?" You tap a sovereign window to assign a self-directed time block. Self-directed goal time is reserved before anything else. Finally, you schedule one maintenance window — 60 to 90 minutes for the life-upkeep tasks that accumulate otherwise.
~10 minutes
7
Session complete
A summary card: your Big Three displayed, sovereign time reserved, maintenance window scheduled, inbox at zero. An encouraging message — varied each week, never repetitive. Then: "See you next Sunday." The session is done. The week has a structure.
~1 minute

Writing good Big Three outcomes

The Big Three are the most important output of the Architecture Session. They shape which tasks surface in your Launchpad all week, what the fit score boosts, and what the system advocates for when other things are competing for your time. Getting them right is worth taking seriously.

Outcomes are results, not activities. "Work on the Henderson proposal" is an activity. "Henderson proposal submitted" is an outcome. The difference matters because one of them tells you when you're done.

The system's hint text reminds you: "Think in outcomes, not activities. What would done look like?" Here's what that means in practice:

1
Chapter 4 first draft complete
Work on the book
✓ Specific, completable, tells you when it's done
2
Friday Airbnb guest fully coordinated — cleaning confirmed, keys ready
Handle Airbnb stuff
✓ Defines the finish line, not just the direction
3
Rest and recover — no work commitments after 4pm this week
Take it easy
✓ Even a rest outcome can be specific — this one has a boundary

Three outcomes is a hard limit, not a suggestion. The constraint is the point. ADHD often produces a planning impulse that generates fifteen "priorities" — which is the same as having none. The Big Three forces a real decision about what this week is actually for. Everything else is held safely in the system and will surface appropriately.

On project linkage

After you write each Big Three outcome, the system looks for matching active projects and offers to link them. Accepting the link means every task inside that project gets a scoring boost for the week — it surfaces more often, earlier, and more prominently in your Now list. If the outcome doesn't connect to a project — "rest and recover this week" — it still anchors the week even without a boost. Linkage is optional but valuable when it fits.

The hat pulse — noticing what's been neglected

Screen 4 is one of the most distinctive features of the Architecture Session. Most weekly reviews ask what you're going to do next week. The hat pulse asks what you've been ignoring.

The neglect detection algorithm computes each hat's status before the session begins, based on how recently tasks in that hat were completed:

Active
Tasks completed this week
Fading
No activity in 7–14 days
Neglected
No activity in 15–29 days
Critical
No activity in 30+ days

Critically neglected hats appear as a warning card at the top of the screen before the full hat list — requiring a response before you can continue. This isn't punitive. It's a signal that something real has gone quiet, and the session is the right moment to notice it.

Each hat has an optional text field. Write something if it helps you think. The notes are stored in your weekly log and visible to your coach if you have one — a week-by-week record of what you were paying attention to and why.

Time sovereignty — paying yourself first

Screen 6 is where the week gets real. Not the intentions — the actual structure.

The problem with most weekly planning is that it happens in the abstract. You write down what you want to accomplish and then discover on Wednesday that the week's calendar left no room for any of it. The obligations came first. The goals got whatever was left. Which was nothing.

The time sovereignty screen inverts this. You see next week's calendar exactly as it is — every external commitment plotted, every sovereign window highlighted. You see the truth of the week before you plan it. Then for each Big Three outcome, a direct question: "When will you work on this?" You tap a sovereign window. A self-directed time block appears. The goals are in the calendar before anything else claims those hours.

The ADHD brain is so pulled by external urgency that self-directed goals are permanently displaced. Time sovereignty is the structural intervention against that drift. Your goals get calendar protection — the same protection your obligations already have.

If you can't find time for one of your Big Three, the screen surfaces that directly: "It looks like this outcome has no protected time this week. Do you want to adjust your commitments, or is this outcome realistic for this week?" This is a real question. If an outcome can't get a single hour of protected time, it may not be a realistic Big Three item for this particular week — and the honest answer is more useful than a plan that was never going to happen.

The maintenance window is a single 60–90 minute block for life-upkeep tasks — household tasks, medical appointments to schedule, errands that have been accumulating. Scheduling it deliberately in the Architecture Session is what keeps maintenance from becoming the reactive flood that drowns the week. One block, once a week, handles it all.

What changes after a completed session

Your Big Three are active from the moment the session completes. Tasks inside linked projects immediately receive a fit score boost — surfacing more often, earlier in the Now list. The time blocks you assigned appear in the calendar and on the time strip. The week goes from a collection of tasks to something with an intention. The daily rhythm is how you execute that intention, one day at a time.

If you miss a week

Missing one session doesn't break your system. Your tasks are still there, your hats are still there, the Launchpad still works. What you lose is the Big Three boost — tasks won't have active weekly targets to connect to. Run the session as soon as you can; even a shortened version of screens 4, 5, and 6 restores most of the benefit. The system doesn't punish the gap. It just waits for you to return.
