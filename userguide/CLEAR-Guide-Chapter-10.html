Tasks in Depth

Chapter 3 covered what happens when you process an inbox item into a task — the eight fields you fill in, the decision you make. This chapter covers everything a task can be after that: its lifecycle states, the additional fields available from the detail view, due dates, dependencies between tasks, and errand runs. Use this chapter as a reference when a specific task feature is unclear.

Task states — the full lifecycle

Every task has a status that tracks where it is in its lifecycle. You don't set these manually — the system manages them based on what happens to the task. Understanding them helps you understand why a task is or isn't appearing where you expect it.

Status What it means
inbox Captured but not yet processed. Sitting in the inbox waiting for a fate decision. Not eligible for the Launchpad.
active Processed and in the live task pool. Eligible for fit score computation. Will surface in the Launchpad when it scores high enough.
pending Recurring task instance that exists in the database but hasn't entered its lead time window yet. Invisible to you — it will become active automatically on the right date.
someday Parked. Real but not timely. Completely inert — won't appear in the Launchpad or affect any score. Surfaces during your monthly Someday review.
done Completed. Removed from the active pool. Time and difficulty logged. Retained as a record.
missed Recurring task instance whose due date passed, plus the grace period, without completion. Archived automatically. The next instance is generated regardless.
deleted Soft-deleted. Gone from all views. Recoverable for 30 days from Settings → Recently deleted, then permanently purged.
template The permanent master record for a recurring task. Holds the recurrence rule and settings. Never appears in the Launchpad — only the instances it generates do.

The full field set

The eight fields you fill in during inbox processing — title, hat, energy, time estimate, who it serves, stakes, location, and activation difficulty — are the minimum. A task has additional fields available from its detail view, most of them optional and most of them adding either context or constraint.

Fields set at processing time

Energy required Deep · Medium · Admin
The cognitive demand of the task — not its difficulty, but how much executive capacity engaging with it requires. A simple phone call can be Deep if the conversation is hard. A complex spreadsheet can be Admin if it's routine mechanical work. Energy match is one of the primary fit score factors.
Time estimate 5m · 15m · 30m · 1hr · 2hr+
Your best guess at how long the task will take. The system compares this against your available time before your next commitment and your personal calibration profile. Tasks whose estimate doesn't fit the available window are suppressed. Estimates improve automatically as the system learns your patterns.
Who does this serve Something I want · Something I have to · Life maintenance
Maps to time_origin internally. Classifies whether this task is self-directed, externally obligated, or life maintenance. Used by the Time Sovereignty map and the "Who Does This Serve?" prompt when a task keeps getting deferred.
Stakes Relational · Functional · Personal
What happens if this doesn't get done. Relational means another person is directly affected — social consequences are immediate. Functional means life degrades without it but consequences are slower-moving. Personal means only you are affected. Stakes influence fit score weighting and grace periods on recurring tasks.
Location context Anywhere · Office · Phone · Computer · Errands · Outdoors · Ranch
Where you need to be to do this. Most tasks are Anywhere and the field can be left at its default. When a task requires a specific context, setting it here means the task only surfaces when you're in that context. Outdoors and Ranch tasks are candidates for weather-aware scheduling.
Activation difficulty Easy · Medium · Hard
How hard is this task to start — subjectively. A hard-to-start task is suppressed when your energy is low. It also makes the Activation Interview available earlier if the task begins to stall. Default is Medium; most people only override this for tasks they know from experience are a struggle to begin.

Fields available from the detail view

Due date
An optional hard deadline. Not prompted during processing — you add one explicitly from the task detail view if it's genuinely needed. Tasks with a due date receive an urgency boost in the fit score as the date approaches. Overdue tasks carry a visual indicator. Use due dates only when there's a real external deadline; adding one to every task undermines the system's ability to distinguish genuine urgency from artificial urgency.
Good use: "File quarterly taxes — due April 15." Bad use: "Reply to Jason's email — due Friday" when Friday is self-imposed.
Depends on
Links this task to another task that must be completed first. A dependent task is completely hidden — from the Launchpad, from project views, from everywhere — until the blocking task is marked done. When the blocker completes, the dependent task becomes active and a notification fires: "You can now start [task title]." Circular dependencies are prevented at creation time. See more on dependencies below.
"Make a key at Home Depot" → "Drop key at the realtor" (the second can't happen until the first is done)
Contact
The primary person this task involves. Linking a contact means the task appears in that person's detail page under "Open tasks" — useful for seeing everything you have in motion with a given person. It also surfaces the contact's context notes in the task detail view as a quiet reminder.
Notes
Free text, rich formatting supported. Shown in the task detail view and in Focus Mode. The place for context, references, or the specific thing you need to remember about how to do this task. Also shown as the "relevant file" surface in Focus Mode when it contains useful content.
Project
Assigns the task to a project. Can be set at processing time or from the detail view later. A task with a project link is part of that project's next action sequence — only the designated next action surfaces in the Launchpad; others are queued behind it.

Due dates — when to use them and when not to

Due dates are the most commonly misused feature in task management systems. The temptation is to add a due date to everything — it creates a feeling of structure and urgency. The problem is that when everything has a due date, nothing is genuinely urgent, and the system loses its ability to distinguish what actually has a deadline from what doesn't.

A due date should mean: something real happens at this date if this task isn't done. A client loses patience. A fine is assessed. A filing is late. If nothing real happens, there isn't a due date — there's a preference.

CLEAR supports due dates but deliberately doesn't prompt for them during processing. You have to opt in explicitly from the task detail view. This is a design choice: the system works harder to protect the signal value of a due date by making it slightly effortful to set one.

When a due date is set, the task receives an escalating urgency boost in the fit score as the date approaches. Within seven days it surfaces more often. Within three days it surfaces prominently. On the due date, it surfaces at the top of the Now list regardless of other scoring factors. If it passes its due date uncompleted, it carries a red date indicator — not as a judgment, but as information.

On invented deadlines

If you find yourself adding due dates to tasks that don't have real external deadlines — "I want this done by Friday" — consider using the Defer feature instead. Deferring to a date moves the task out of your active pool until that date arrives, without adding false urgency. The task reappears when you said you wanted it to, without telling the system it's urgent when it isn't.

Dependencies — when one thing must happen first

Some tasks can't start until another task is done. Dropping the key at the realtor's office depends on having made the key first. Sending the proposal depends on finishing the cost estimate. CLEAR models this with a dependency link — one task referencing another as its blocker.

Setting a dependency is straightforward from the task detail view: tap "Depends on," then select the blocking task from your active pool. The dependent task immediately disappears from the Launchpad and all task views. It exists in the database but is completely invisible until the blocker is completed.

When the blocking task is marked done, the dependent task activates automatically. A notification fires: "You can now start [task title]." The task enters the active pool and will surface in the Launchpad when the fit score puts it there.

Errand runs handle dependencies automatically. When you group tasks into an errand run and one has a dependency on another task in the same run, the system places the dependent task after its blocker in the sequence and prevents it from being reordered before it. You don't need to set a dependency link manually for tasks in the same errand run — the sequence handles it.

When to use dependencies vs. project sequence

If two tasks are both part of a project, the project's next action sequence already handles ordering — only the current next action surfaces at any given time. Dependencies are most useful for tasks that aren't in the same project but have a real-world ordering constraint. "Make key" and "Drop key at realtor" might be two separate tasks in different hats — the dependency link is what connects them.

Errand runs — batching tasks by location

When you have multiple tasks that share the same location context, running them together as a single outing is almost always more efficient than doing them separately. Errand runs are how CLEAR supports this.

Without errand runs, four errand tasks compete individually in your Now list — getting displaced by higher-scoring tasks from other hats, surfacing at separate times, requiring you to mentally reassemble them into a coherent outing. With an errand run, they're grouped into a single Launchpad entry that surfaces when you have the time and context for the whole outing.

How they're created

The system detects potential errand run candidates automatically: when three or more active tasks share the same location context and their combined estimated time is under three hours, a suggestion appears on the Launchpad: "You have 4 errands. Want to plan them as a run?" One tap accepts. Declining is equally prominent — no pressure.

You can also create a run manually from any task row via long-press → "Group into errand run," or by selecting multiple tasks from the task list and tapping "Create errand run."

The errand run planner

When you accept or create a run, the Errand Run Planner opens. It shows all candidate tasks for the location context, pre-selected. You can deselect any you don't want in this run, drag to reorder the sequence, and give the run a label — "Saturday errands," "Hardware run," "Ranch morning."

Dependencies within the run are enforced automatically: a task that depends on another in the same run is placed after its blocker and can't be dragged before it. A small arrow connects them visually. The estimated total time appears at the bottom, updating as tasks are added or removed.

On errand run timing

An errand run surfaces in the Launchpad when its combined time fits a sovereign window and your location context is set to match. If you're at the ranch and the run is tagged "errands," it won't surface — the system knows you're not in the right place. Change your location context to "errands" when you're ready to go out, and the run will appear.

A few things worth knowing

The MIT flag

Every task has an is_mit field — a boolean that marks it as today's Most Important Task. Only one task can be MIT at a time. The flag is set during the morning setup or the daily close, and cleared when the task is completed or when a new MIT is designated. You don't set this field directly — it's set by the MIT selection flow in those two rituals.

Sort order

When you manually reorder tasks in the Launchpad via long-press drag, the new sequence is stored in the sort_order field. It persists until tomorrow's first Launchpad render, at which point the system resumes control. The "Custom order — resets tomorrow" indicator is visible when a manual order is active.

Tags

Tasks support an optional tags field — a freeform list of text labels. Tags aren't used by the fit score algorithm and don't drive any system behavior. They're available for personal organization if you find them useful, and can be searched from the task list view. They're entirely optional and most users don't use them.

Soft deletes

Deleting a task sets its status to deleted rather than removing it from the database. Deleted tasks are invisible in all normal views but recoverable for 30 days from Settings → Recently deleted. After 30 days they're permanently purged. This applies to all task types including recurring instances — deleting an instance doesn't delete the template or affect future instances.
