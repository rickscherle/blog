Your Morning Setup

Before the Launchpad opens each day, CLEAR asks you three quick questions. The whole thing takes about two minutes. What it does with your answers shapes everything about how your day unfolds.

When it runs

The morning setup runs automatically the first time you open CLEAR each workday, at or after the start of your configured workday hours — the default is 8:00 AM. It only runs once per day. If you've already completed it and come back to the app later, you go straight to the Launchpad.

If you open CLEAR before your workday hours begin — checking your inbox late at night, capturing a thought early in the morning — you'll see the late capture screen instead. The morning setup waits until the day has actually started.

If you skip it

You can dismiss the morning setup and go straight to the Launchpad. The system will use a default energy level of Medium and carry forward yesterday's MIT if it wasn't completed. The setup is useful, but it's not a gate — nothing breaks if you skip it on a rushed morning.

Screen 1 — Your energy level

The first screen is a single question: "How's your energy right now?" Four options. You tap one and move on.

This is the most important input in the entire setup, and probably the most counterintuitive one. Most productivity systems ask what you need to do. CLEAR starts by asking who you are right now — because the right task depends entirely on who's doing it.

A 90-minute deep writing session is not the same task for someone at peak energy as it is for someone running on four hours of sleep. The words are the same. The person is different. The system needs to know which person it's working with today.

The four energy levels and what each one means in practice:

🔥
High — Ready to go deep
You're at your best. The system will prioritize your most cognitively demanding tasks — deep work, writing, complex problem-solving — while this window lasts. Don't waste high energy on admin.
Deep work tasks surface first
⚡
Medium — Can focus but not for long
A solid but not exceptional state. The system surfaces tasks that require real concentration but are completable in shorter sessions. Most days land here. It's a perfectly good place to do real work.
Balanced mix of focus and admin tasks
🌊
Low — Need easy wins today
Your capacity for sustained attention is limited today. The system shifts to lower-friction tasks — shorter, more concrete, less cognitively demanding. Easy wins still count. Progress is still progress.
Short, concrete tasks prioritized
🧩
Fragmented — Scattered day of small windows
You have real commitments today and your available time comes in small chunks — ten minutes here, fifteen minutes there. The system surfaces tasks that can be started and completed in those windows without requiring you to rebuild context each time.
Quick-complete tasks and phone calls prioritized

Your energy selection persists for the whole day. If your state changes mid-day — a bad meeting drains you, or an afternoon coffee kicks in — you can update it at any time by tapping the energy indicator in the Launchpad header. The Now list recalculates immediately.

On honesty

The energy check only works if you answer honestly. There's a strong temptation to select High regardless of how you actually feel — it seems like the aspirational choice. Resist it. The system isn't judging you. It's trying to match tasks to the person who's actually going to do them today. A Low energy day that's used well is better than a High energy selection that produces paralysis.

Screen 2 — Heads up for the next 3 days

After you set your energy, a brief awareness screen appears before you confirm your MIT. It shows anything time-sensitive in the next 72 hours: appointments, meetings, reminders firing soon, events approaching, and recurring tasks coming due within their lead time.

The screen is purely informational. Nothing on it requires a decision. It doesn't ask you to act on any of the items — it just makes sure you've seen them before the day starts. A vendor call tomorrow morning, Sarah's birthday on Friday, a bill due Thursday — these things appear here so they're not surprises when they arrive.

The items are ordered by proximity: things happening today appear at the top and are slightly brighter. Things further out appear below in more muted text. Today's items carry a small teal dot — a quiet signal that these need your attention before the day is out.

Tap "Got it" and the screen closes. That's the only interaction. You've been briefed. The day can start.

The awareness strip at the bottom of the Launchpad (described in Chapter 5) shows a subset of the same information — the two or three most time-sensitive items — throughout the day. The morning heads-up gives you the full 72-hour picture once. The strip keeps the most pressing items visible as a quiet reminder.

Screen 3 — Confirming your MIT

The final screen of the morning setup shows your Most Important Task for today.

If you set tomorrow's MIT during last night's Daily Close, that task is waiting here. The system shows it prominently with a brief rationale: why it was selected, which weekly outcome it's connected to, and how long it's estimated to take.

If no MIT was set last night — if you skipped the Daily Close, or if this is your first day using CLEAR — the system suggests one based on your Big Three outcomes for the week, anything overdue, and what it knows about your calendar today.

Confirming

One tap to confirm. The MIT is set. You'll see it on the Launchpad for the rest of the day. The decision is made. You don't have to think about it again until you start working on it.

Swapping

If the suggested MIT isn't right — if something more urgent has come up overnight, or if the suggestion doesn't match what you know about today — you can swap it. Up to five alternatives are shown below the primary suggestion, filtered by your current energy level. Tap any of them to make it your MIT instead.

The alternatives aren't random. They're chosen from your active task pool using the same fit score logic that drives the Launchpad — energy match, time available before your first commitment, and connection to your weekly outcomes. A Low energy selection will show different alternatives than a High energy selection.

What makes a good MIT

The best MIT is specific, completable in one focused session, and connected to something that genuinely matters this week. "Work on the book" is a poor MIT — too vague to start. "Write the opening scene of Chapter 4" is a good one. If the suggested MIT feels vague or unclear, swap it or edit it before confirming. A clear MIT is worth the extra thirty seconds it takes to get right.

What the system does with your answers

Your energy selection, the heads-up acknowledgment, and your MIT confirmation aren't stored as data points and forgotten. They actively shape what you see for the rest of the day.

Energy feeds directly into the fit score algorithm — the system that decides which tasks appear in your Now list and in what order. A High energy selection boosts deep work tasks and suppresses quick admin. A Low selection does the reverse. A Fragmented day surfaces tasks that can be completed in under fifteen minutes without requiring context ramp-up. Every time the Launchpad recalculates — which happens automatically throughout the day as time passes and tasks are completed — your energy setting is one of the primary inputs.

The heads-up does something subtler. The items you were shown are now in the awareness strip at the bottom of the Launchpad. You saw the full 72-hour picture at the start of the day; the strip keeps the most time-pressing items visible as a quiet reminder throughout the day without having to re-brief you.

Your MIT sits at the top of the Launchpad all day, separate from the Now list, in its own banner. It doesn't compete with other tasks for position — it's always there, always first, always visible. The system is not going to let you forget what you said mattered most today.

The whole setup takes about two minutes on a focused morning. Less, once you know what you're doing. That's a reasonable price for a day that starts with direction rather than a blank slate.
