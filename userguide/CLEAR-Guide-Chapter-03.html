Processing Your Inbox

Processing is the act of deciding what each captured item actually is and where it belongs. It's not complicated — there are six possible outcomes and the system walks you through one at a time. But it matters more than any other habit in CLEAR, because it's the bridge between capturing a thought and actually doing something with it.

What processing means

The inbox is a holding area, not a destination. Everything that lands there is waiting for a decision. Processing is making that decision — quickly, specifically, one item at a time — so that things move out of the inbox and into the right place in your system.

An item sitting in the inbox isn't costing you much. An inbox that never gets processed is costing you everything — because captured thoughts that never get resolved are still taking up space in your head, and that's exactly what the capture system was supposed to fix.

The goal isn't to process your inbox the moment something arrives. The goal is to process it regularly enough that the inbox never becomes a place you're afraid to open. Most people find that two or three short processing sessions a week — ten to fifteen minutes each — is enough to keep it manageable. The Weekly Architecture Session always includes an inbox-to-zero step. Between sessions, things wait.

How to open a processing session

Tap any item in your inbox list to open it. A bottom sheet slides up showing the full text of the item — or the image, voice note, or attachment if that's what was captured. Below it, the six fate options appear as large tappable cards.

You make one decision. The item resolves. The next item slides up automatically if you choose to continue, or you return to the inbox list. Either way, the badge count decrements immediately — the system acknowledges the decision the moment you make it.

Creating a task — the eight fields

The Task path is the most common and the most thorough. When you choose it, a short guided flow walks through eight fields, one at a time. Each field occupies its own screen — no crowded form, no decision fatigue from looking at too many things at once.

The eight fields exist because the system needs real information to do its job. A task with no energy level, no time estimate, and no hat is inert — it can't surface correctly in the Launchpad because the fit score algorithm has nothing to work with. The fields aren't busywork. They're the difference between a task that surfaces when it should and one that sits in the database doing nothing.

  • 1
    Title — rewrite it as a concrete action
    The raw capture text is shown editable. Rewrite it as a specific physical action — a verb, a clear outcome. "Water pump noise" becomes "Call Henderson HVAC to schedule pump inspection." The more specific the title, the easier the task is to start when it surfaces. Vague titles are one of the most common causes of task stall.
  • 2
    Hat
    Which life area does this belong to? One tap on your hat list. The hat determines the color coding throughout CLEAR and feeds the neglect detection system.
  • 3
    Energy required
    Deep, Medium, or Admin. Not the objective difficulty of the task — your subjective energy requirement to engage with it. A five-minute phone call can be a Deep task if it's a hard conversation. Energy determines when the task surfaces relative to your daily energy selection.
  • 4
    Time estimate
    Five tappable options: 5m, 15m, 30m, 1hr, 2hr+. Your best guess. It doesn't have to be exact — the system calibrates against your actual completion times over time and gets better at accounting for the gap. But some estimate is required. A task with no time estimate can't be matched to available windows.
  • 5
    Who does this serve?
    Something I want to do, Something I have to do, or Life maintenance. This maps to whether the task is self-directed, externally obligated, or purely a maintenance function. The fit score uses this to protect sovereign time and to surface the "Who Does This Serve?" prompt if a task keeps getting deferred.
  • 6
    What happens if it doesn't get done?
    Someone else is affected (relational), Life gets harder (functional), or Just me (personal). Stakes determine priority weighting. A task that affects another person surfaces with more urgency than one that only affects you.
  • 7
    Where do you need to be?
    Anywhere, Office, Phone, Computer, Errands, Outdoors, or Ranch. Most tasks are Anywhere — the chip is pre-selected and you skip this field with one tap. But if a task can only be done at a specific place, set it here. Location context is how the system surfaces the right tasks when you're out running errands or at the ranch.
  • 8
    How hard is this to start?
    Easy, Medium, or Hard. Not difficulty — activation energy. A hard-to-start task doesn't surface when your energy is depleted. Knowing something is a hard starter also makes the Activation Interview available earlier if the task begins to stall.
On due dates

Due dates are not one of the eight standard fields. They're available via a small "Add a deadline" link at the bottom of the flow, but they're deliberately opt-in. Most tasks don't need a due date — prompting for one creates artificial urgency and trains you to treat everything as if it's overdue. Use due dates for tasks with real external deadlines. Leave them off everything else.

The eight fields cover what a task needs to be created. Tasks also support contacts, file attachments, dependencies on other tasks, errand run membership, and project linkage — all of which can be added from the task detail view at any point. The complete task layer is covered in Chapter 10 — Tasks in Depth.

When the system knows what it is

When you capture something that contains clear date and time language — "dentist Thursday March 20th at 2pm," a forwarded meeting invitation, a voice note that includes a specific time — the system detects it before you reach the fate decision. When the processing sheet opens, Appointment is already selected and the date and time fields are pre-filled.

You can override the pre-selection if the detection is wrong. But most of the time it's right, and for these items the processing flow compresses significantly — title, hat, duration, and you're done. The appointment goes straight to the calendar.

This is the first step toward the "Do" capture intent described in Chapter 2. Auto-detection doesn't process the item for you — you still confirm the fields — but it removes the decision about what kind of thing it is, which is often the hardest part.

When the inbox gets away from you

There will be weeks where you don't keep up. Life is busy, sessions get skipped, and the inbox grows. Most productivity systems respond to this by showing you a long list of everything you haven't processed, which is exactly the kind of view that produces ADHD paralysis. CLEAR responds differently.

When your unreviewed inbox count exceeds ten items, a "Clear the Deck" button appears at the top of the inbox. Tapping it gives you three options:

🧹 Quick Pass — pick through them fast
Items are shown one at a time with just two options: Keep or Toss. No hat, no energy, no fields — just: is this real or not? Kept items move to your Processing Queue (confirmed real, full processing later). Trashed items are soft-deleted. One second per item, ten items maximum per pass — the rest go to the queue automatically.
📦 Park them all in Someday
Every unreviewed item becomes a Someday task. Nothing is lost — everything is recoverable during your monthly Someday review. Use this when you genuinely don't have the bandwidth to process right now but want to stop the inbox from haunting you.
🗑 Trash them all
Everything is soft-deleted. A confirmation tells you exactly how many items will be trashed and reminds you they're recoverable for 30 days. Use this when a long period of unmaintained capture has produced a backlog that's mostly noise.

The Processing Queue — items marked Keep during a quick pass — shows up as a quieter secondary badge in the inbox. They're confirmed real, waiting for the full setup. They surface prominently during the Weekly Architecture Session's inbox step, which treats them as priority items before fresh captures.

Why bulk "create as tasks" isn't an option

You might expect a "Turn everything into tasks" option — but it doesn't exist. A task with no hat, no energy, and no time estimate can't surface correctly in the Launchpad. The fit score algorithm has nothing to work with. Bulk-creating tasks without those fields would produce a pile of inert records that never appear when they should. That outcome is worse than leaving things in the inbox. The quick pass is the fastest path that keeps the data useful.

After processing

The moment a task is processed and marked active, it's eligible for the Launchpad. The system doesn't wait — the next time the Now list renders, your new task is in the pool. If it fits the current energy, time window, and context, it may appear immediately.

The inbox item itself doesn't disappear from the database. It's retained as a provenance record — evidence of when and how the item entered the system, including the original voice note if that's how it was captured. You'll never see it again in normal use, but if you ever need to know "when did I capture this?" or "what was the original text?", the record is there.

Processing is a skill that gets faster with practice. The first few times through the eight-field task flow, it might feel like a lot of decisions. Within a week it becomes automatic — the fields follow a logical sequence and most of the answers come without much thought. The payoff is a task list where everything is specific, contextualized, and actually usable.
