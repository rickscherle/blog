Your Inbox

The inbox is where everything starts. Not just tasks — thoughts, ideas, voice notes, forwarded emails, scanned documents, anything. If something occurs to you that might matter, it goes here first. No decisions at capture time. Just get it out of your head and into the system.

What the inbox is for

The ADHD brain generates a constant stream of thoughts — obligations remembered mid-conversation, ideas arriving at inconvenient moments, tasks surfacing while you're doing something else entirely. Most systems ask you to decide what something is before you can capture it. CLEAR doesn't. The inbox is a pure holding area. Capture now, decide later.

The goal at capture time is one thing: get it out of your head before it disappears. Nothing else matters at that moment — not organization, not priority, not even whether it's actually important.

The inbox accumulates everything. You process it separately, on your own schedule — a few times a week, during your Weekly Architecture Session, or whenever you have ten minutes and the mental bandwidth to make good decisions. The capture and the decision are intentionally separated because they require different cognitive states.

Six ways to get something in

CLEAR is designed so that capture is never more than a tap away, regardless of what you're doing or which device you're using.

🎙
Voice — in app
Tap the microphone on the capture sheet. Recording begins immediately — no confirmation, no mode selection. Speak naturally. On release, your audio is transcribed to text and the item lands in your inbox with both the transcript and the original audio retained. If the transcription confidence is low, the item is flagged for review.
Fastest for driving, cooking, or any moment when your hands aren't free.
⌨️
Typing — in app
Tap the floating + button from any screen. The capture sheet slides up with a text field. Type what's on your mind, tap Save or Do, done. The sheet closes and you're back where you were. Everything else in the app stays in place — context preserved.
Best for specific, precise captures where wording matters.
✉️
Email forwarding
At setup, CLEAR gives you a personal capture address — something like [email protected]. Forward any email there and it lands in your inbox. The subject line becomes the headline, the sender appears as a subtitle, and any attachments — PDFs, images, documents — are stored alongside the item and queued for text extraction. The system will suggest a normalized task title if the email looks like an action item.
Ideal for emails that need to become tasks without getting lost in your mail app.
📤
Share extension
From any app — Safari, Notes, Maps, a news app, anything — tap the share button and choose CLEAR. The content of whatever you're looking at — a webpage, an address, an article, a screenshot — goes straight to your inbox. You don't need to switch apps, copy anything, or break your flow.
Great for "I need to do something with this" moments while browsing.
📄
Document scan
CLEAR monitors a designated folder in your cloud storage — the folder your printer or scanner deposits files into. When a new document appears, it's automatically captured to your inbox as an attachment, OCR'd, and flagged for processing. Physical paperwork — receipts, medical forms, contracts, handwritten notes — flows into the system without any manual steps.
Set it up once during onboarding and paper stops being a separate pile.
🗣
Voice — without opening the app
On iPhone: "Hey Siri, add to CLEAR inbox: [your thought]." On Android: a Google Assistant action does the same. On Apple Watch: tap the CLEAR complication and speak — your paired iPhone handles transcription and sync without you touching the watch screen. None of these require the app to be open, or even your phone to be in your hand.
The fastest path when a thought arrives and your phone is in your pocket.

No decisions at capture time. None of the capture methods ask you to pick a hat, assign a priority, or decide what kind of thing this is. That all happens later, during processing. The capture sheet has two buttons — Save and Do — and that's the only choice required. Everything else waits.

Save versus Do

When you capture something in the app, two buttons appear at the bottom of the capture sheet: Save and Do.

Save
Goes straight to the inbox. No AI, no processing, no inference. The thought is safe in under two seconds. Review it later when you have the bandwidth to make good decisions. This is the offload path — get it out of your head, deal with it later.
Do
Signals that you want to act on this now, not later. The system reads what you've entered, infers what kind of thing it is, and routes it appropriately — creating a task, appointment, or reminder with the information it can determine from your capture. Use this when the thought is already complete enough to act on.

The distinction matters. "Pick up a cake at Hansen's Bakery tonight at 6pm" is complete enough for Do — the system can detect the time and create an appointment immediately. "Call the attorney about the filing" is not — too many missing details. Save it, process it properly later, and the processing flow will ask for what's needed.

When in doubt, Save. The inbox will hold it faithfully until you're ready. There's no penalty for using the safe path.

Items captured by email show the subject line and sender. Items captured by voice show a microphone icon alongside the transcript.

Gestures

Two swipe gestures work on any inbox item. Swipe left opens the processing sheet — the same as tapping the item. Swipe right trashes the item immediately with no processing required. Use the right swipe when something is obviously junk — a captured thought that was already irrelevant by the time you got here.

The inbox badge

The badge on the CLEAR app icon and the People tab shows the count of unreviewed items — things that have arrived but haven't been touched yet. Items you've been through during a quick pass (confirmed as real, waiting for full processing) are tracked separately as a quieter indicator: "12 items to process when you're ready." These don't add to the urgent badge.

On inbox anxiety

A growing inbox badge can become a source of stress, particularly if you associate it with falling behind. The badge represents things waiting for a decision — not things you've failed to do. An inbox of 25 items is 25 captured thoughts that are safely stored. That's a good thing. Process them when you're ready, not when the badge makes you feel bad.

What happens when something arrives

When an item enters your inbox — however it got there — it sits in unreviewed status. It's inert. It doesn't affect your task list, your Launchpad, or anything else in the system. It's waiting.

Email forwarded items and document scans begin background processing immediately: attachments are extracted, OCR runs on PDFs and images, and if the email looks like an action item the system quietly suggests a normalized task title. None of this requires your attention — it's just making the item easier to process when you get to it.

Voice captures are transcribed immediately. If the transcription confidence is below the system threshold — a noisy environment, a fast talker, an unusual word — the item is flagged with a small indicator: "We weren't sure we caught that right — check this one." The original audio is always retained alongside the transcript.

Nothing is lost. Nothing expires. Items sit in the inbox until you decide what to do with them.

A habit worth building

The inbox works best as a capture-now, process-later system. The capture half requires almost nothing — a tap, a few words, a voice note. The processing half requires real cognitive engagement, and that's why they're separated.

Most people who use CLEAR find that two or three short processing sessions a week is enough. The Weekly Architecture Session always includes an inbox-to-zero step as one of its five components — so even if you never open your inbox between sessions, it will get cleared once a week at minimum.

The capture habit — the reflex to externalize a thought the moment it arrives — is the thing worth practicing. Everything else in CLEAR depends on the inbox being fed. The more reliably you capture, the more reliably the system can do its job.
