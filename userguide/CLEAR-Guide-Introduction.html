Part One

The problem isn't you. It's the tools.

I spent most of my life knowing I was smart. I could see problems clearly, generate ideas quickly, and talk about what needed to be done with real precision. What I couldn't do — what baffled me and everyone around me — was actually do the things I said I was going to do. Not the big things. The ordinary things. The Tuesday afternoon things.

I wasn't diagnosed with ADHD until I was in my late fifties. And the diagnosis didn't just explain my struggles — it reframed my entire life. The piles on my desk, the unfinished projects, the promises I'd made and hadn't kept — these weren't character defects. They were symptoms of a neurological condition that nobody had thought to look for until it was almost too late to matter.

The person with ADHD isn't lazy or undisciplined. They have a real neurological impairment in the brain's self-management system — and they've been expected to perform as if it works perfectly.

The clinical researchers who have shaped our understanding of this condition — Dr. Russell Barkley, Dr. William Dodson, Dr. Julia Ross among them — have built a remarkably clear picture of what ADHD actually is. Barkley describes it not as an attention disorder but as a disorder of self-regulation and working memory, combined with a phenomenon he calls time blindness. The future doesn't feel real. Deadlines two weeks away are motivationally invisible. The only time that carries any weight is right now.

I want to be honest: I'm not claiming any affiliation with these researchers. They don't know this system exists. But their work is the reason it does. They gave me the map of what's actually broken — and that map is what allowed me to build something that compensates for each deficit directly.

Why the tools always fail

Every major productivity system on the market — Todoist, Things, Notion, paper planners, GTD — was designed by and for neurotypical people. They assume you can do things that ADHD specifically impairs:

  • Hold time in your mind
    These systems show you clock times — "meeting at 2pm." But time blindness means "2pm" has no felt weight until it's 1:58 and you're in the wrong building.
  • Choose from a long list
    A 47-item to-do list is not a productivity tool for someone with ADHD. It's a wall of equally urgent noise that produces paralysis, not action.
  • Self-initiate without a prompt
    These systems wait for you to remember to check them. But the entire problem with ADHD is that you can't reliably remember to check anything.
  • Recover after falling off
    When you miss a day — or a week — conventional systems confront you with a backlog of failure. That shame spiral makes picking the system back up feel impossible.
  • See the whole task, not just the visible tip
    Every task is an iceberg. You plan for the part above water — the dinner itself, the meeting, the phone call. But beneath the surface: preparation time, travel there, travel back, follow-up. Conventional systems show you the tip. The rest stays invisible until it capsizes your day.

The abandoned to-do app isn't evidence that you're broken. It's evidence of a product-market mismatch. The tool was built for someone else's brain.

Part Two

A different approach, built from the neuroscience up

I built CLEAR after my diagnosis because I couldn't find anything that actually addressed what my brain needed. Not a better task list. Not a prettier calendar. A system that understood the specific way ADHD impairs function and compensated for it directly.

A note on framing before we go further — because it shapes everything that follows.

You may have heard ADHD described as a "different way of thinking" or a "superpower." That framing is well-intentioned. But we have to be honest with you: ADHD is a real neurological impairment. It causes real suffering. Describing it as merely a cognitive style, however kind the intention, can discourage people from seeking the treatment and accommodations that would materially improve their lives. And CLEAR is built on honesty, not comfort.

The best analogy I've found

I've tried many ways to explain what ADHD actually is — to people who have it, and to the people around them. The one that finally works: CLEAR is eyeglasses for your brain.

Think about what happens to a child with uncorrected nearsightedness in the early grades of school. They sit in class, they can't read the blackboard clearly, and they fall behind. But here's what makes it particularly cruel: they don't know why. They can't see what they can't see. Nobody around them knows why either — they look fine. They seem like they're paying attention. The teacher thinks they're not trying hard enough. The parents think they're distracted. The child thinks there must be something wrong with them.

That happened to me. Not with ADHD — with actual vision. I spent early years of school at a disadvantage and didn't know it. I thought that's what school was like. I thought everyone was squinting at the board.

Then I got glasses. And the world snapped into focus.

The experience of undiagnosed ADHD is almost exactly this. You're sitting in the classroom of your own life — struggling in ways you can't fully articulate, falling behind in ways others can't fully see, and not understanding why. The people around you see someone who's intelligent, who clearly understands things when they're explained, who seems capable — and can't figure out why they can't just do the ordinary things. You can't figure it out either.

CLEAR is eyeglasses for your brain. Not a cure. Not a character upgrade. A compensation for a real structural deficit — one that is invisible to the people around you, and often invisible to yourself.

Here's why I keep coming back to this analogy:

Glasses don't cure your vision. You will wear them for the rest of your life. CLEAR doesn't cure your ADHD. The underlying neurology doesn't change. The system is there every day because the deficit is there every day — and that's okay.

Glasses may not get you to 20/20. They correct, not cure. CLEAR won't make you neurotypical. It will make you dramatically more functional than you are without it. That's the goal, and it's worth wanting.

Poor vision really is a handicap. Not a "differently sighted" perspective — a genuine impairment that puts you at a real disadvantage without correction. People are entirely comfortable saying this about vision. They are far less comfortable saying it about ADHD. The analogy makes that inconsistency visible. If it would be absurd to tell someone who can't see the blackboard to "try harder" or "pay more attention," it is equally absurd to say it to someone whose executive function doesn't reliably show up.

Glasses are a perfectly ordinary, widely accepted prosthetic. Nobody thinks glasses are cheating. Nobody believes you'd be fine without them if you just focused more. Nobody says "well, some people see just fine without glasses, why can't you?" The accommodation is so normalized that we've forgotten it's an accommodation. That's where we need to get with ADHD.

More specifically: CLEAR acts as what I think of as an external prefrontal cortex — the part of the brain responsible for self-regulation, planning, working memory, and initiating action. When yours doesn't reliably show up for work, the system shows up instead.

The CLEAR framework

The system is organized around five principles that correspond to the five letters:

C
Capture Everything
A single inbox where everything goes immediately — by voice, text, email, or photo. Your brain stops holding things and starts processing them.
L
Limit Ruthlessly
Once a week, you set three — and only three — outcomes that would make the week a success. Not tasks. Outcomes. The system holds everything else.
E
Execute on Context
The system knows your energy level and available time and shows you only the tasks that fit right now. No decisions. Just start.
A
Account for Time
Time is made physical and visible. Countdowns replace clock times. Your sovereign time — the hours that are genuinely yours — is shown on every screen.
R
Review and Reset
A ten-minute daily close resolves every open loop before you sleep. A weekly planning session sets the week's intention. The system never lets things fall through the cracks.
Part Three

What it actually looks like to use it

Here's what a day with CLEAR feels like in practice.

The moment a thought hits you

It's 7am. You're making coffee and you remember that the ranch water pump has been making a noise. In the past, that thought would live in your head all day, generating background anxiety, until you either dealt with it urgently or forgot it entirely.

With CLEAR, you say: "Hey Siri, add to CLEAR inbox: water pump on the ranch is making a grinding noise, probably needs service before winter." Done. The thought is out of your head and safe in the system in under ten seconds. You go back to your coffee.

Opening the app during your workday

It's 9:15am. You open CLEAR. The first thing you see is not a list of 47 tasks. You see one thing: your Most Important Task for today — the one thing that, if you do nothing else, makes this a successful day. Below it, a handful of tasks filtered to match your energy and the time you actually have before your next commitment.

The two horizontal bars below the MIT banner are your day laid out physically. The white vertical line is now. The teal segments on the lower bar are windows of time that genuinely belong to you — no meetings, no obligations. At a glance, you can see that you have about seven and a half hours of sovereign time today. Not all in a row — but real, usable time.

Ending the day with a clear head

At 5:30pm, CLEAR sends you a notification: "Time to close out the day. Ten minutes and your head will be clear." You run through a five-screen flow. You log what you actually did, route anything incomplete to tomorrow or someday, and — critically — you decide right now what tomorrow's Most Important Task will be.

When you're done, the system tells you: "You're finished for today. The system will hold everything until morning." The badge clears. The app goes quiet. Tomorrow's first decision is already made.

For someone with ADHD, that last part is not a small thing. The overnight anxiety of unresolved open loops is one of the most debilitating features of the condition. Your impaired working memory can't discharge incomplete tasks, and your impaired emotional regulation turns each one into an affective burden. The tasks don't just sit in your mind. They hum. The daily close is a deliberate cognitive offloading ritual: you transfer the contents of your working memory to an external store, and the system gives you explicit permission to disengage.

Part Four

What changes when the system works

I want to be honest with you: CLEAR is not a cure. ADHD is neurological, and no app changes your neurology. What CLEAR does is provide the external scaffolding that compensates for the internal scaffolding that isn't reliably there.

The thing nobody talks about: shame

Before the benefits, I need to name the thing that may be doing you more damage than the ADHD itself: shame.

In my experience — and in the clinical literature — shame is the single most destructive secondary effect of ADHD. The internalized belief, built over years and decades of repeated failure, that you are fundamentally unreliable. That your word doesn't mean anything. That the gap between what you intend and what you deliver is a moral failure rather than a neurological one.

Every system you've ever tried has reinforced that shame. You set it up with hope, used it for four days, fell off — and when you opened it again, there was a wall of overdue tasks staring back at you. Evidence. Proof that you failed again. So you closed the app, deleted it, and added one more entry to the long private catalog of things that didn't work because of you.

CLEAR was designed to break that cycle at the architectural level. When you miss a day, or a week, the system doesn't confront you with a backlog of failure. It simply meets you where you are and asks: "What matters today?" This is not a small design choice. It is a deliberate intervention against the shame spiral that has been reinforced by every productivity tool, every disappointed teacher, every broken promise, for your entire life. The system will not punish you. That pattern ends here.

Here's what changes when the external scaffolding is actually in place:

The background anxiety quiets
When everything is captured and held safely, you stop carrying it in your head. The low-level hum of "what am I forgetting" starts to fade.
Time becomes visible
The time strip makes the shape of your day physical. You stop being surprised by how little time you have — and by how much you actually do.
Your own goals get protected
The system advocates for your self-directed work, not just your obligations. The book doesn't keep getting crowded out by everyone else's urgencies.
Falling off doesn't mean failing
When you miss a day or a week, the system recovers gracefully. No streak counters. No backlog of shame. Just a clean path forward from wherever you are.
Part Five

How to use this guide

This guide has 21 chapters organized into six parts. You don't have to read them in order — and most people won't. But the first few chapters build on each other, and if CLEAR is new to you, reading Parts One and Two sequentially will give you a much better foundation than skipping around.

4–8

A note on CLEAR-specific language

CLEAR uses a few terms you won't find in other systems. They appear throughout the guide and will become natural quickly, but the first time you encounter them they may feel unfamiliar.

Hats are what other systems call areas, domains, or life categories — the distinct roles you play and contexts your life runs in. We call them hats because the metaphor is right: you wear different hats in different parts of your life, and the person wearing the Airbnb hat is genuinely in a different headspace than the person wearing the Book hat.

Sovereign windows are blocks of time that genuinely belong to you — no meetings, no obligations, nothing external making a claim on them. They appear on the time strip as teal segments. The distinction between sovereign time and committed time is one of the foundational ideas in CLEAR.

The Now list is the short list of tasks on the Launchpad — not all your tasks, just the ones that fit your current energy and available time. The system picks them; you do them.

The MIT is your Most Important Task — the one thing that would make today a success if nothing else happened. You set it each morning (or the night before). It lives at the top of the Launchpad all day.

Anything else that's unfamiliar is defined when it first appears, and there's a full glossary in the Appendix.

Part Six

A note to the people who love someone with ADHD

If you're reading this because someone in your life has ADHD — a partner, a child, a parent — I want to say something directly to you.

What you've been watching isn't laziness. It isn't indifference. It isn't a choice. The person you love is running a race in shoes that don't fit, on a track that wasn't designed for them, and the cruelest part is that they look fine from the outside. So the failure is attributed to character rather than circumstance.

CLEAR can help — but it is not a solution you can give someone. It is a system a person has to choose. The most useful thing a loved one can do is understand what the condition actually is, stop interpreting failure as a moral statement, and be curious rather than frustrated when the system doesn't immediately fix everything.

And I want to acknowledge something else: your experience of living with someone who has ADHD is real and valid too. The frustration, the sense of being an unpaid project manager, the erosion of trust when promises are broken again — these are legitimate responses to a real problem. You are not being unreasonable. You are living with the downstream effects of a neurological condition that neither of you asked for.

There is a specific way this system can help your relationship: for the first time, you will both be looking at the same honest picture of what is happening. Not your interpretation of it. Not their memory of it. The actual record. This matters because one of the most corrosive dynamics in ADHD relationships is what clinicians call the perception gap — the ADHD partner genuinely believes they're doing more than the neurotypical partner observes, and both are telling the truth from their own cognitive vantage point. That gap breeds resentment on both sides. The shared data resolves it without either person being the villain.

That's enough preamble. The system is waiting for you. Chapter 1 is where we set it up.
