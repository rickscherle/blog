The Daily Close

The Daily Close is a ten-minute ritual at the end of your workday. It resolves the open loops from today, handles anything that didn't happen, and makes one decision about tomorrow before you stop. When it's done, the app goes quiet and you're free.

Why it matters

For most people with ADHD, the end of the day is when the anxiety starts. The work is over — or at least the official workday is — but the mental load doesn't stop. The unfinished tasks, the commitments not logged, the things that probably need to happen tomorrow but haven't been decided yet — they all sit in working memory overnight, generating a low-level hum of worry that disrupts sleep and bleeds into the next morning.

The Daily Close exists for one reason: to move everything out of your head and into the system before you go to sleep, so the system holds it and you don't have to.

This isn't a productivity technique. It's a neurological one. Research on ADHD and sleep consistently finds that unresolved open loops — things the brain hasn't been given permission to stop thinking about — are a significant contributor to the sleep difficulties that affect a large proportion of ADHD adults. The Daily Close gives the brain an explicit signal: this is handled, you can let it go.

The second thing it does is make tomorrow's first decision tonight. By the time you open CLEAR in the morning, your Most Important Task is already set. You don't face a blank slate and have to figure out where to start — you already know. That matters more than it sounds.

When it appears

CLEAR sends a notification at your configured end-of-workday time — the default is 5:30 PM, and you can change it in Settings. The notification says: "Time to close out the day. Ten minutes and your head will be clear."

You can also start the Daily Close manually at any time by tapping the close icon on the Launchpad. If you're done at 3pm on a slow day, you can close then. If you're still working at 7pm, close when you're actually done.

What if you miss it?

Missing a Daily Close doesn't break anything. The streak counter resets, but the system doesn't punish you. Tasks that weren't handled simply stay where they are. When you open the app the next morning, the Launchpad picks up where it left off. If you've been away for several days, the re-engagement flow handles the backlog gracefully — that's covered in Chapter 19.

The five screens

The Daily Close runs as a five-screen flow. Each screen has one job. Together they take about ten minutes, though experienced users often move through them faster.

1
What did you actually do?
The tasks from today's Launchpad are shown one by one. For each one, a quick tap: Done, In Progress, or Didn't happen. Nothing more is asked at this stage. A running tally at the bottom fills in as you go — "3 done, 1 in progress, 1 didn't happen." This screen is logging, not judging. You're just recording what happened.
~3 minutes
2
Handle the incompletes
For each task marked "Didn't happen," you make one decision. Four options — no open-ended questions, no blank fields. Pick one and move on.
~3 minutes
3
Tomorrow's MIT
The system suggests tomorrow's Most Important Task based on your Big Three outcomes, anything overdue, and what's on your calendar. You confirm it or swap it for something else. Either way, by the time this screen is done, tomorrow's first decision is made. The screen ends with: "Done — your first task tomorrow is already decided."
~2 minutes
4
One sentence
Optional. A single text field: "How was today?" One sentence, free text, completely skippable. If you write something, it gets stored in your daily log — visible to you later, and to your coach if you have one. If you skip it, no judgment, no follow-up. Some people write here every day. Some never do. Both are fine.
~1 minute (optional)
5
Close complete
A brief summary: tasks completed, MIT set for tomorrow. Then the sign-off: "You're done for today. The system will hold everything until morning." The inbox badge clears. The app goes quiet. Nothing will interrupt you until your next workday starts.
~30 seconds

Handling incomplete tasks

Screen 2 is the only place where a genuine decision is required. For each task that didn't happen today, you pick one of four options:

🌅 Tomorrow
Moves it to the top of tomorrow's active pool. Use when you genuinely intend to do it first thing.
📅 Defer
Moves it back into active circulation without any particular urgency. It'll surface again based on the fit score.
📦 Someday
Parks it. Real but not timely. You'll see it again in your monthly Someday review.
🗑 Delete
It wasn't actually important. Soft-deletes the task. Recoverable for 30 days if you change your mind.

The four options are deliberately limited. There's no "remind me in three days" or "assign to someone" — those things belong in the task detail view, not here. The Daily Close is about resolution, not task management. Each decision should take about three seconds.

The "In Progress" tasks from Screen 1 don't appear here. A task marked In Progress is still active — it remains in your task pool exactly where it was, with whatever focus session time has been logged. You don't need to do anything special with it. Tomorrow it'll surface again when the fit score puts it there.

Setting tomorrow's MIT

Screen 3 is the most consequential screen in the entire Daily Close. By the time you tap through it, you've already made the most important decision of tomorrow.

The system suggests a candidate MIT based on three things: what your Big Three outcomes are for the week, what's overdue or approaching a deadline, and what's on your calendar tomorrow that might constrain your available time. The suggestion is shown with a brief rationale: "Suggested because it's linked to your Big Three and hasn't had any progress this week."

You confirm it with one tap. Or you swap it — a short list of alternative candidates is shown below the suggestion, filtered by tomorrow's energy context if you've provided it. You tap the one you prefer. That's it.

This decision is worth taking seriously. The MIT shapes everything about how tomorrow starts. A good MIT is specific, completable in one focused session, and connected to something that genuinely matters. A vague MIT — or one chosen carelessly — leads to a morning where you open the app and don't quite know what you're supposed to be doing.

If you're not sure what tomorrow's MIT should be, the best question is: what would make tomorrow feel like a success even if nothing else got done? That's the task. Not the most urgent, not the most overdue — the one that would actually matter if it happened.

The one-sentence note

Screen 4 is optional and brief. A text field, a placeholder: "How was today?" One sentence. Skip it if you want.

People who use this regularly tend to find it unexpectedly useful — not as a diary, but as a pattern detector. After a few weeks, the daily notes start to reveal things: which kinds of days feel good, which kinds feel draining, what the common threads are in the difficult ones. The Weekly Architecture Session can surface patterns from your daily notes. Your coach, if you have one, can see them.

But none of that requires writing anything. The one-sentence note is an invitation, not an obligation.

What "closed" means

When the Daily Close completes, a few things happen at once. The inbox badge clears — not because the inbox is empty, but because CLEAR's job for today is done and you don't need to see it until tomorrow. The Launchpad goes into a quiet evening mode. The coaching pulse disappears. The floating capture button stays — because thoughts don't respect workday hours — but everything that would normally prompt action is dimmed or hidden.

The app is still there. You can still capture things. But the operational layer steps back. If you open CLEAR at 10pm, you see the late capture screen from Chapter 2, not the Launchpad. The system is in holding mode.

Tomorrow morning, when your workday hours begin, everything comes back. The Launchpad opens with your pre-decided MIT at the top. The morning energy check runs. The day starts from a position of clarity rather than chaos — because the clarity was built the night before.

The ten minutes you spend on the Daily Close tonight buys you something you can't manufacture in the morning: the knowledge that everything is handled, the first decision is already made, and you can actually sleep.
