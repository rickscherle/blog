Salad Dressings · Part Two

The Goddess Was Always Green

A dressing born in one of San Francisco's grandest hotel kitchens, forgotten for a generation, and now — finally — back where it belongs.

A Great Recipe Worth Knowing

There is a moment in a good restaurant kitchen when someone sets a bowl of Green Goddess dressing in front of you and the color alone stops the conversation. It is the green of new leaves in April, of a garden just after rain. It doesn't look like salad dressing. It looks like something a chef made because they couldn't help themselves.

Which, as it turns out, is almost exactly how it came to exist.

The year was 1923. San Francisco's Palace Hotel — already one of the grand hotels of the American West, rebuilt and reopened after the 1906 earthquake destroyed the original — was hosting a theatrical celebrity. George Arliss, a British actor at the peak of his fame, was appearing in a play called The Green Goddess, a melodrama set in the Himalayas that had become a sensation on both sides of the Atlantic. Arliss stayed at the Palace for an extended run, and the hotel's executive chef, Philip Roemer, did what great hotel chefs have always done for important guests: he named something after him. Or rather, after his show.

The dressing Roemer created was lush and herbaceous — mayonnaise and sour cream as the base, brightened with tarragon, parsley, and chives, deepened with anchovy, sharpened with lemon and white wine vinegar.

It was blended smooth and served on butter lettuce at a time when most American salads were dressed with bottled Italian or plain oil and vinegar. It was, by the standards of 1923, almost radical.

The play closed. Arliss moved on. Green Goddess did not.

For decades it was a fixture of fine dining and supper clubs, the kind of dressing that appeared alongside the shrimp cocktail and the wedge salad in white-tablecloth restaurants across the country. Then, as American tastes shifted in the 1980s and '90s — toward fat-free everything, toward balsamic, toward Caesar — Green Goddess quietly stepped back from the center of the table.

It never went away. It just waited.

The dressing's revival over the last decade has been genuine and well-deserved. Food writers rediscovered it, restaurants put it back on menus, and then social media got hold of it. A TikTok trend turned it into a phenomenon — millions of views for people blending what amounts to a simplified Green Goddess and pouring it over shredded cabbage and cucumber. The irony is that the viral version stripped out the tarragon, the anchovy, and half the complexity that made the original worth caring about. The people making it from pantry staples were getting a taste of the idea. The real thing is considerably better.

And the real thing, as it happens, is not difficult to make. A blender. A handful of fresh herbs. Ten minutes, plus a half hour in the refrigerator while the flavors decide to get along.

The Recipe

Green Goddess Dressing

The Palace Hotel's legendary herb dressing, restored to its rightful glory. Makes about 1 cup — enough to dress salads for 6 to 8.

Ingredients

  • ½ cup mayonnaise
  • ¼ cup sour cream
  • ¼ cup fresh tarragon leaves*
  • ½ cup fresh flat-leaf parsley
  • 3 tbsp fresh chives
  • 3 anchovy fillets, oil-packed
  • 1 clove garlic
  • 2 tbsp lemon juice, fresh
  • 1 tbsp white wine vinegar
  • ½ tsp kosher salt
  • ¼ tsp black pepper, fresh ground

Method

  1. Prep the herbs. Wash and thoroughly dry the tarragon, parsley, and chives. Wet herbs will water down your dressing. A salad spinner followed by a pat with paper towels does the job.

  2. Build the base. Add the mayonnaise, sour cream, anchovy fillets, and garlic clove to a blender or food processor. Pulse a few times to get things moving.

  3. Add the green. Add the tarragon, parsley, and chives along with the lemon juice and white wine vinegar. Blend on high for 30 to 45 seconds until completely smooth and brilliantly green.

  4. Season and taste. Add salt and pepper. Taste and adjust — more lemon for brightness, more vinegar for edge, more salt to bring everything forward. Blend briefly to incorporate.

  5. Chill. Transfer to a jar and refrigerate at least 30 minutes before serving. The flavors marry and the color deepens. Keeps refrigerated for up to five days.

* A Note on Tarragon

Tarragon is the herb most likely to be missing from a typical supermarket produce section — and it is the one you cannot skip. That faint anise quality is what gives Green Goddess its character. Parsley is the body, chives are the freshness, but tarragon is the soul of this dressing.

If your market doesn't carry it fresh, check the spice aisle. Two teaspoons of dried tarragon is the right substitution — add it directly to the blender with the mayo and sour cream, rather than with the fresh herbs, so it has a moment to hydrate before blending. The result is not identical to the fresh version, but it is honest and good.

Alternatively, a combination of fresh basil and a tablespoon of fennel fronds — if your store carries fennel bulbs — comes remarkably close to tarragon's flavor profile and makes a beautiful dressing in its own right.

Notes from the Kitchen

On the anchovies: Don't skip them and don't announce them. They dissolve completely into the dressing and provide the savory depth that makes people ask what your secret is. For a vegetarian version, a teaspoon of white miso is a reasonable substitute — different, but it works.

Serving suggestions: Butter lettuce or romaine hearts. Also exceptional as a dip for grilled artichokes, a sauce for cold poached salmon, or spooned over a halved avocado.
